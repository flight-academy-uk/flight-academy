## Context

What is the situation that requires a decision? What constraints apply? What did we know at the time? What did we not know?

Keep this factual. Avoid arguing for the eventual decision here — that comes later. State the forces at play, the problem that needs solving, and any constraints (regulatory, technical, organisational, budgetary) that bound the solution space.

## Decision

What did we decide? State the choice in one sentence at the top of this section, then expand with detail.

Be specific. Avoid hedging language ("we may want to consider…") — if a decision is conditional, state the condition explicitly. The reader in two years should be able to read this section and know exactly what was decided and what is in force.

## Consequences

### Positive

- What this enables
- What problems it solves
- Costs it avoids

### Negative

- What this rules out
- What complexity it introduces
- What it makes harder to change later

### Neutral

- Side effects that are not clearly positive or negative
- Trade-offs accepted as part of the decision

## Alternatives considered

For each significant alternative we considered and rejected:

### Alternative N — name

Brief description. Why was this rejected? What would have changed our mind?

Be honest. The point of recording alternatives is so a future contributor can re-evaluate when conditions change. If we rejected something because it was new and untested, say so — that may no longer be the case when someone reads this in 2030.
